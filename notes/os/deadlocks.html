Deadlocks in Operating Systems

October 4, 2025

A deadlock is a state in an operating system where two or more processes are waiting for resources that are held by each other, and none of them can proceed. This results in an indefinite waiting condition that halts system progress.

1. Necessary Conditions

For a deadlock to occur, four conditions must hold simultaneously:

  • Mutual Exclusion: At least one resource must be non-shareable.
  • Hold and Wait: A process holding one resource is waiting for others.
  • No Preemption: Resources cannot be forcibly taken away.
  • Circular Wait: A cycle exists where processes wait on each other’s resources.

2. Deadlock Handling Methods

There are four major strategies to handle deadlocks:

  • Deadlock Prevention: Ensure one of the four conditions never holds.
  • Deadlock Avoidance: Use algorithms like Banker’s Algorithm to avoid unsafe states.
  • Deadlock Detection: Allow deadlocks to occur but detect and recover later.
  • Deadlock Ignorance: Simply ignore (used in many OS like Linux, assuming deadlocks are rare).

3. Real-World Example

Consider two processes: one holding a printer and waiting for a scanner, while another holds the scanner and waits for the printer. Neither can proceed—this is a classic deadlock.

Summary

Deadlocks are critical to understand for ensuring efficient resource management in operating systems. By learning prevention, avoidance, detection, and recovery techniques, we can design safer and more reliable systems.
